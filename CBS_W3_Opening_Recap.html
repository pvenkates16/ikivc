The skill that matters is recognising the shape of a situation — so that’s what we’re practising.

Recognise the shape (1/2)

No law names given. Which pattern fits, and why?

1. A support team is measured on “tickets closed per hour.” Within a month, agents close tickets fast without fixing the issue. Ticket volume looks great. Complaints quietly rise.

Goodhart’s / Campbell’s Law family — the metric (tickets closed) became the target, and the team optimised for the metric instead of the real goal.

2. A data migration is given 8 weeks and takes exactly 8 weeks. The same migration, done elsewhere under real time pressure, took under 2 weeks with similar scope.

Parkinson’s Law — work expands to fill the time available, regardless of how much is actually needed.

3. A bank’s disaster-recovery failover has never been tested under real conditions in 3 years. The one time the primary system goes down, the failover also fails to trigger.

Murphy’s Law — anything that can go wrong will go wrong, especially the untested safeguard nobody has exercised.

4. A software rollout is 3 months behind. Leadership adds 15 engineers to catch up. Six weeks later, it is 5 months behind.

Brooks’s Law — adding people to a late project makes it later, since onboarding and coordination overhead outweighs the added capacity.

Recognise the shape (2/2)

5. A multinational’s fraud-detection reporting comes out in three inconsistent formats, one per regional office. The company itself operates as three loosely-coordinated regional divisions.

Conway’s Law — the system’s structure mirrors the communication structure of the organisation that built it.

6. In 2015, executives claim a new technology will transform the industry within 2 years. By 2017, headlines call it a failure. By 2025, it quietly underpins most of the industry’s infrastructure, with little fanfare.

Amara’s Law — we overestimate the short-run impact of technology, and underestimate its long-run impact.

7. A centralised compliance team of 5 people reviews every transaction across a bank with 40 million customers and thousands of edge cases. Approvals slow to a crawl and errors increase.

Ashby’s Law of Requisite Variety — the controller (a small, simple team) is not complex enough to absorb the variety of the system it is trying to control.

The habit to build: read the situation first, name the shape second — not the other way round.

Deep dive

Types of Bias — Spot the Pattern

You already know AI inherits bias from its training data. Today is about which mechanism — because “there’s bias” is the first sentence of a diagnosis, not the diagnosis itself.

Type 1 — Historical / Label bias

The training label — not the inputs — already encodes a past biased decision.

Case F3, Amazon AI Hiring Tool

“Hired / not hired” encoded a decade of the company’s own historical bias. No audit of whether the labels themselves were unbiased before training began.

Case F4, Dutch Childcare Benefits

Nationality used as a risk signal. Correlation existed because enforcement had always targeted immigrant communities — the model amplified existing bias that was already in the historical enforcement pattern.

Quick-fire diagnosis

A hospital trains a model to predict which patients need extra care, using past healthcare spending as the label for “need.” Sicker patients from lower-income groups historically spent less — not because they were healthier, but because they had less access to care. What type of bias is this, and where exactly does it live: the inputs, or the label?

This is label bias. It lives in the label, not the inputs: past spending was used as a stand-in for “medical need,” but spending reflected access to care, not actual need.

Type 2 — Proxy / Measurement bias

Excluding a sensitive attribute as an input does not stop correlated features from reconstructing it anyway.

Case F3, Amazon — continued

“When corrected for the specific gendered words, it found other proxies for gender.”

Case F4, Dutch Childcare Benefits — same case, different angle

Nationality was never meant to stand in for anything — here it was used directly. The broader pattern it illustrates: a feature correlated with a protected group can carry that group’s risk profile even when unintended.

Quick-fire diagnosis

A car insurer removes gender from its pricing model but keeps “occupation” and “vehicle type.” Six months later, pricing still splits cleanly along gender lines. What happened?

This is proxy bias. Occupation and vehicle type correlate closely enough with gender that the model reconstructs the excluded variable indirectly, without ever seeing it directly.

Type 3 — Feedback loop / Amplification bias

The model’s own outputs become tomorrow’s training data, so early bias compounds instead of self-correcting.

Case F13, YouTube Recommendations

“Feedback Loop Amplification: Higher engagement → more recommendations → higher engagement. System locks onto conspiracy pathways.” Watch time was optimised; user welfare was not.

Quick-fire diagnosis — same shape, different industry

A lending model rejects Group A at a higher rate. Rejected applicants never get a loan, so there is no repayment outcome to record for them. Next month’s retraining data has proportionally fewer Group A success stories to learn from. What is happening, and how is it the same shape as YouTube’s failure?

This is feedback loop bias. Same shape as YouTube: the system’s own past outputs shrink or distort what future training data can show, so the model becomes more confident in its original judgement purely from its own prior decisions — not from new independent evidence.

Type 4 — Representation / Sampling bias

No label is biased and no proxy is smuggling anything in — a group is simply underrepresented in the training data itself.

Case NM3, Google Photos

“The model learned from data that underrepresented darker skin tones. The failure was baked in before a line of production code was written.” Removing the label afterward did not fix the underlying misclassification — it just hid it.

Quick-fire diagnosis

A voice assistant trained mostly on one accent group performs noticeably worse for speakers of other accents, even though nothing in the training process ever labelled or targeted any accent. Why does this count as its own type, distinct from Type 1 or Type 2?

This is representation bias. There is no biased label (Type 1) and no correlated proxy smuggling in an excluded variable (Type 2) — the training data itself simply under-samples a group, so the model never learns that group well in the first place.

Recognition drill

For each one-liner: which type, and why — in one sentence.

1. A resume screener trained on 10 years of “who got promoted” keeps favouring one demographic, even with names removed.

Historical / label bias — the promotion outcome itself encodes past decisions.

2. A loan model excludes postcode, but still approves one area far less — because “years at current address” correlates with it.

Proxy / measurement bias — a correlated feature reconstructs the excluded variable.

3. A content-moderation model flags one group’s posts more; those posts get removed and can never be reviewed as “fine after all” in future training data.

Feedback loop bias — the model’s own past removals shape what future training data can even show.

4. A medical imaging model performs worse on scans from one type of equipment, simply because the training set had far fewer scans from that equipment.

Representation / sampling bias — no biased label or proxy involved, just an underrepresented slice of the training data.
Deep dive, continued

Types of Proxy Creep

You already know Proxy Creep as a Week 2 pattern: “when a proxy metric completely replaces the messy reality it was supposed to track.” Today: three distinct shapes that takes.

Type 1 — Activity proxy

“We did the activity” is mistaken for “we verified it worked.”

Case F1, Knight Capital

“'Deployment done' used as proxy for 'deployment verified.' Eight servers updated felt like all eight servers updated.” No automated test confirmed it.

Case F2, Starbucks / NomadGo

“'Improved inventory management' used as proxy for 'system works.'” No staged pilot against documented accuracy benchmarks before scaling to 11,000 locations.

Quick-fire diagnosis

A team migrates a database over the weekend. Monday’s status report says “migration complete.” Nobody actually queried the new database to confirm the data matches the old one. What proxy is being trusted here?

An activity proxy: completing the migration task is being treated as proof the migration worked, with no independent check in between.

Type 2 — Output proxy

“The system produced an output” is mistaken for “the output is correct.”

Case F7, Air Canada chatbot

“'Chatbot answered the question' as proxy for 'chatbot gave correct answer.'” The chatbot generated plausible text, not verified policy — no retrieval layer grounded it in the real bereavement policy document.

Quick-fire diagnosis

A GenAI tool drafts a legal summary that reads fluently and confidently. The team ships it because it “looks right.” Nobody checked it against the source contract. What proxy is being trusted here?

An output proxy: a fluent, confident-sounding output is being treated as a correct one, with no grounding check against the actual source document.

Type 3 — Self-reported metric proxy

A party measures its own performance, and that self-measurement stands in for independent validation.

Case F8, Pieces Technologies

“A self-defined hallucination rate became proxy for safety... Pieces measured itself and marketed the result.” No industry standard, no third-party audit.

Case F15, Theranos

“Information Asymmetry: claim revolutionary, hide details as 'trade secret,' so nobody can verify.” A different industry, the identical shape.

Quick-fire diagnosis

A vendor advertises “99% accuracy” for its fraud-detection product, based on tests it designed and ran itself. No customer or regulator has independently reproduced the number. What proxy is being trusted here?

A self-reported metric proxy: the vendor’s own measurement of its own product is standing in for independent validation, with no external party checking the claim.

Recognition drill

For each one-liner: which proxy type, and why — in one sentence.

1. A team rolls out a security patch to every server on their list and closes the ticket. Nobody confirms the patch is actually running on each server.

Activity proxy — rolling out the patch is treated as proof it is installed and working everywhere.

2. A translation AI produces a fluent, natural-sounding sentence. It is published without anyone who speaks the target language checking it against the original meaning.

Output proxy — fluent output is treated as accurate output, with no check against the source meaning.

3. A startup publishes its own internal benchmark showing its model beats competitors, using a test set it designed and never released publicly.

Self-reported metric proxy — a self-designed, self-graded benchmark stands in for independent, reproducible validation.
Deep dive, continued — Governance · Quantum · Blockchain

System Property vs. Requirement Collisions

Four recurring shapes: an inherent property of a system runs into an external requirement. Same shapes, different domains — spot the collision, not the vocabulary.

Type A — Permanence vs. the right to change

Commonly known as: Immutability vs. the Right to Erasure

A system cannot un-write something, but a rule says it must be changeable.

Course material

Hash chain: “Change one block → chain breaks” — any edit is detectable, which is exactly why it cannot be quietly undone. GDPR Article 17: a data subject can request erasure of their personal data.

Quick-fire diagnosis

A hospital keeps patient consent records on a public blockchain to prove nothing was tampered with. A patient later withdraws consent and asks for their record to be deleted. What collision is this, and why can’t the hospital simply delete the block?

Permanence vs. the right to change. The chain’s core value — tamper-evidence — is structurally the same feature that blocks deletion: altering or removing a block breaks the hash link for every block after it.

Type B — Anonymity vs. accountability

Commonly known as: Pseudonymity vs. Identification

A system does not require identity, but a rule says someone must always be identifiable.

Course material

Public blockchains: anyone can join the network and hold a wallet, with no central approval and no identity check required.

Quick-fire diagnosis

A charity wants to accept donations through a public blockchain for transparency. Anti-money-laundering rules require it to verify the identity of anyone donating above a set threshold. What collision is this?

Anonymity vs. accountability. Wallets do not require identity, but the law requires it above a threshold. A fix: only require identity verification for donations crossing that threshold, keeping the public chain for everything below it.

Type C — Present security vs. a future-dated break

Mechanism’s accepted name: Harvest Now, Decrypt Later

Something is safe today by assumption — but that assumption has a known expiry date.

Course material

“Harvest now, decrypt later”: an adversary can intercept encrypted data today, store it, and decrypt it years from now once quantum computers are powerful enough. RSA is vulnerable to Shor’s algorithm.

Quick-fire diagnosis

A government seals classified documents today using standard RSA encryption, planning to declassify them in 40 years. Why is “RSA is currently unbroken” not a sufficient safety argument here?

Present security vs. a future-dated break. Harvest now, decrypt later means an adversary can copy the encrypted documents today and simply wait. A 40-year retention window is easily long enough for quantum computers to reach the power needed to break RSA.

Type D — Necessity vs. overhead

Known framework: “Do You Need a Blockchain?” (Wüst & Gervais, 2018)

Blockchain’s properties solve a real problem only when parties do not already trust each other.

Course material

The hash chain, distributed ledger, and consensus layers exist to solve specific weaknesses: single points of failure, counterparty risk, and tampering — problems that come from not knowing or trusting the other party.

Quick-fire diagnosis

Three sister companies under the same parent firm want a shared ledger to track internal inventory transfers between each other. They propose a blockchain. What should you ask first?

Necessity vs. overhead. Ask whether a shared database would deliver the same benefit, since all three companies already share a parent and already trust each other structurally. Blockchain’s value comes from removing the need for trust among independent, potentially adversarial parties — which does not clearly apply here.

Recognition drill

For each one-liner: which collision, and why — in one sentence.

1. A land registry records property titles on a public chain for transparency. A court later orders a fraudulent title transfer to be reversed. The registry cannot simply edit the record.

Permanence vs. the right to change — the same immutability that makes the registry trustworthy also blocks the court-ordered correction.

2. An art marketplace lets anyone trade digital collectibles anonymously. Tax authorities require the marketplace to report who earned capital gains above a threshold.

Anonymity vs. accountability — anonymous trading conflicts with a legal duty to identify and report specific parties.

3. A pharmaceutical company encrypts 30-year drug trial data with today’s standard encryption, assuming it is secure for the full retention period.

Present security vs. a future-dated break — today’s encryption being unbroken says nothing about whether it stays unbroken for 30 more years.

4. Two departments in the same company, who already share one internal database, propose migrating to a blockchain "for better trust" between them.

Necessity vs. overhead — the departments already trust each other and share infrastructure, so blockchain's decentralised-trust value proposition is not solving a problem they actually have.

Make it visceral: Annoyance vs. Disaster

Forget the formulas for a second. Ask one question: which mistake would you rather make?

FALSE POSITIVE = THE ANNOYANCE
You cried wolf. Someone double-checks, rolls their eyes, moves on. Mildly costly. Rarely dangerous.
FALSE NEGATIVE = THE DISASTER
The real thing happened, and nobody caught it. The cost shows up later, and it’s bigger.
The thumb rule

If a miss (FN) is the disaster and a false alarm (FP) is just annoying — chase recall. Cast a wide net; tolerate noise. | If a false alarm (FP) is the expensive or embarrassing one and a miss (FN) is tolerable — chase precision. Only act when sure.

Thumb rules: numerators and denominators

ACCURACY
(TP + TN) / everything
Denominator = all four boxes. “How often was I right, overall?”
PRECISION
TP / (TP + FP)
Denominator = everything YOU flagged (the predicted-positive column). “Of my calls, how many were right?”
RECALL
TP / (TP + FN)
Denominator = everything REALLY there (the actual-positive row). “Of what was real, how much did I catch?”
The one-line thumb rule

Prec-ISION is about your own calls — a column question. Re-CALL is about reality — a row question. Same TP numerator, always. The denominator tells you which one you’re asking.

Quick-fire: Annoyance or Disaster? Chase precision, or recall?

1. A wildfire alert system decides whether to trigger evacuation warnings for a region.

Chase recall. A missed fire (FN) is the disaster — people could die. An unnecessary evacuation (FP) is costly and annoying, but survivable.

2. A spam filter protecting a small business’s inbox, where every incoming email could be a paying customer.

Chase precision. Here, the false positive is the disaster — a real customer email wrongly marked spam and never seen. A missed spam email (FN) getting through is just annoying.

3. A data centre’s overheating alarm, deciding whether to trigger an automatic shutdown.

Chase recall. A missed real overheat (FN) risks destroyed hardware or fire — the disaster. An unnecessary shutdown (FP) costs downtime — annoying, not catastrophic.

Notice #2 flips the pattern. The lesson isn’t “always chase recall” — it’s “find out which mistake is the disaster, every single time.”
